oasis7 / Roadshow Deck

An AI-agent civilization sim where history keeps moving after you leave

The world lives in a fractured asteroid belt. The player does not take over units directly. Instead, you operate from outside the civilization, set goals and constraints, and watch agents push expansion, production, trade, alliances, and war forward under real pressure.

Genre Persistent civilization sim, not a one-shot AI demo
Player position Outside-the-world strategist, not a unit-by-unit micromanager
Current stage Public technical preview: explainable and testable, but not a mass-play release
This deck is the sequenced presentation layer. It does not replace the homepage boundary or the PRD source of truth.

01 / Agenda

Why this is worth another look

This story moves through 5 questions

  1. What kind of game is this, beyond AI wrapping?
  2. Why could this kind of world hold attention longer than a normal demo?
  3. How do the player, agents, and world rules divide responsibility?
  4. What already exists today, and what still does not?
  5. What has to be made solid next?

Deck Boundary

This solves sequence, not total detail

The homepage and the long overview are better for free browsing. The value of a deck is that it can carry the problem, world, product boundary, and stage judgment in one controlled order.

02 / Problem

Many AI game demos fail not because the model is weak, but because the world is soft

A

They can talk, but they do not pay consequences

Characters sound smart, but their behavior is not bound by resources, time, law, or state truth.

B

They look like worlds, but behave like scripts

There is no continuously running timeline and no reliable state change that can be replayed or audited.

C

The player ends up pulling puppet strings

If the player makes every real decision directly, the world itself never becomes autonomous enough to matter.

`oasis7` starts from a different question: how do you make the world itself strong enough to hold history?

03 / Thesis

The core product thesis: build the world first, then let civilization grow out of it

World-first

All valid behavior goes through world rules No skipping runtime, no silently writing outcomes into the table.

Persistent

The world keeps running and history does not reset on logout Organizations, production lines, alliances, and conflicts all stay on the same timeline.

Emergence-first

The project cares more about what rules produce than about pre-written plot Markets, protocols, organizations, and war outcomes should grow from in-world behavior.
The target is not “slightly smarter NPCs.” The target is a world that can accumulate history.

04 / Player Role

You set civilization direction, but you do not take over every unit

01

You set strategy

Expand through the belt, stockpile and fortify, or push for governance and protocol power.

02

You set boundaries

You provide goals, constraints, and governance rules instead of prescribing every move.

03

Agents execute

They produce, trade, ally, and fight under resource, time, and legality pressure.

04

The world records consequences

Prices, logistics, alliances, and governance proposals turn your inputs into inspectable history.

05 / Why It Hooks

What makes this compelling is not clicking buttons. It is watching the situation change shape on its own.

Resources tighten first

One broken mining line can lift prices, destabilize production, and turn neighbors into hoarders or blockers.

Alliances negotiate before they break

Pressure often starts as trade, bluffing, and temporary deals before it turns into defection and re-alignment.

War leaves a long tail

Conflict is not a single animation. Routes stay broken, policy shifts persist, and the same world remembers.

Core Feeling

Watching disorder harden into order

The real payoff is not “I clicked a lot.” It is “one judgment I made kept rewriting the expansion, risk, and tempo that came afterward.”

06 / Stack

The stack is not there to impress. It is there to make world state hold.

LLM Agents
WASM Modules
World Runtime
Consensus Layer
Distributed Storage & Networking
LLM handles decision space But it does not directly become world truth.
WASM carries higher-order capability Institutional, industrial, and gameplay logic can ship as modules.
Runtime + consensus carry truth State change needs verification, not just a convincing single-machine story.

07 / What Exists Today

Today it is explainable and testable, but it should not be packaged as a public-play release

Already exists

  • A public homepage and long-form overview that explain the project clearly
  • A docs hub, viewer manual, testing manual, and public skill entry
  • Technical-preview validation paths across runtime, viewer, and testing
  • Static GitHub Pages publishing with site quality gates

Current boundary

  • It is still not a mainstream player-ready product
  • It is better described as researchable, testable, and alignable technical preview work
  • The deck is here to explain that boundary, not to polish it away
Public claim stays the same: `technical preview / not playable yet`.

08 / Path

The path comes in two phases: make one world solid first, then make it into a broader substrate

Phase 1

Open-world sandbox game

First make the feeling of participation, exploration, and consequence worth staying for in one persistent world.

  • Make the gameplay loop stand up
  • Make world-side consequence chains legible
  • Compress technical preview into a tighter bounded experience

Phase 2

Open-world platform

Then evolve it into a substrate that can hold different rule sets, module ecosystems, and participant roles.

  • Creators become more than consumers
  • Rules and play styles continue to evolve
  • But that is not the current public promise

09 / Next Focus

The next stage is not about piling on more concept. It is about making the experience loop more solid.

Gameplay loop Make the chain from resource pressure to alliance shift to war consequence more reliably felt by the player.
Experience entry Keep tightening the preview entry so it is easier to understand, easier to verify, and harder to misread.
Proof chain Keep public narrative aligned with repo truth instead of letting docs patch over over-claims.
At this stage, the most valuable thing is not stronger language. It is stronger proof that the world is already moving.

10 / Close

One-line close

`oasis7` is not trying to be a pile of talkative characters. It is trying to become a world where civilization history keeps advancing under resource, time, law, and consensus constraints.
